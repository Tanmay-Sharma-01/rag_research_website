RAG System for TBI Speech‑Language Pathology Support

A retrieval-augmented generation pipeline designed to bridge the gap between neurological research and the full ecosystem of TBI care — clinicians and caregivers alike.

01 — Project Overview

Two problems, one system

TBI rehabilitation creates a knowledge crisis on two fronts: clinicians can't keep pace with research, and families are left without the information they urgently need.

GitHub Repo Poster Board

The Clinical Gap

Therapists must manually sift through a vast, ever-growing body of scientific literature, creating a significant lag between new discoveries and their clinical application. Traumatic brain injury rehabilitation is a complex, interdisciplinary process that demands constant access to the latest neurological research.

The Caretaker Gap

TBI recovery involves family members and caretakers who need to understand the patient's condition in plain, accessible language — a group frequently left without meaningful information, increasing caretaker stress and slowing recovery at home.

Our Contribution

This system was developed to address both challenges simultaneously, serving as a novel contribution to AI-assisted rehabilitation that supports the full ecosystem of care around a TBI patient — not just the clinical team. Built under the Given Voice Program at Penn State's College of Information Sciences and Technology, the project demonstrates that meaningful personalization in clinical AI is achievable without model fine-tuning.

02 — Research Questions

Three questions drove everything

1
Can a RAG system effectively surface relevant TBI research for both clinical and non-clinical users?
2
Does integrating patient-specific context via physician's notes improve the relevance and usefulness of AI-generated responses?
3
Can the system meaningfully close the knowledge gap between medical professionals and family caretakers of TBI patients?
03 — Technical Architecture

A full RAG pipeline in four stages

Built entirely in Python with a focus on system autonomy — no external API calls at the embedding stage means full privacy compliance and offline capability.

STAGE 01

Paper Ingestion

Neurological literature collected from curated PDFs and PubMed Central via E-utilities API. A keyword co-occurrence scoring mechanism prioritizes clinically relevant papers. Chunked into 500-character segments with overlap.

PubMed API PMC efetch
STAGE 02

Embedding & Indexing

All document chunks embedded using sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2 — running fully locally and offline. Stored in ChromaDB for efficient semantic retrieval with zero external API dependency.

HuggingFace ChromaDB
STAGE 03

Semantic Retrieval

User queries trigger a semantic search over the ChromaDB index, retrieving the top-5 most contextually relevant document chunks. Retrieval-first grounding directly reduces hallucination risk common in purely generative models.

Vector Search Top-5 Chunks
STAGE 04

Response Generation

Retrieved chunks pass to DeepSeek LLM alongside the user's query. Each answer includes source attribution so users can trace which research papers informed the output. FastAPI backend + login-protected frontend.

DeepSeek FastAPI
Literature Base

~300

research papers at poster presentation, with infrastructure to scale to 1000+ articles

Key Design Principle

Embedding runs fully offline — no external API calls at the indexing stage. This was a deliberate decision for system autonomy and compliance with health data privacy requirements.

04 — Key Feature

Physician's Note Personalization

The system's defining feature emerged directly from caretaker feedback — a design that proves meaningful personalization is achievable entirely within a RAG framework, with no fine-tuning required.

Optional Note Upload

Users can attach a physician's note or clinical summary alongside their query. This document is incorporated directly into the retrieval context, enabling responses tailored to a specific patient's situation.

Privacy by Design

Users retain full control over what clinical information they choose to share. The design preserves privacy and maintains compliance with relevant health data policies — no data is stored post-session.

No Fine-Tuning Required

Meaningful personalization is achieved entirely within the RAG framework. This demonstrated that expensive, compute-intensive model training is not a prerequisite for contextually relevant clinical AI responses.

User-Centered Origin

This feature emerged directly from iterative end-user feedback during evaluation sessions, underscoring the value of a user-centered design methodology in clinical AI development.

05 — Evaluation & Results

Four key findings

The system was evaluated through a structured feedback session with a real patient's caretaker, who queried the system on topics directly relevant to their caregiving situation.

Finding 01 — Usability

Highly Accessible to Non-Professionals

The caretaker found the system highly usable and effective for quickly accessing information about the patient's TBI condition. Non-professional users without clinical training were able to retrieve meaningful, readable content with minimal friction.

Finding 02 — Information Relevance

General Guidance Had Limits

While system responses were consistently accurate and grounded in indexed research, general TBI guidance was sometimes less actionable than desired given the nuances of the unique caregiving context. Personalization emerged as the primary improvement area.

Finding 03 — Personalization as Solution

Physician's Note Closed the Gap

The relevance gap identified in Finding 2 directly motivated the optional physician's note upload feature. After implementation, the system produced noticeably more relevant, specific, and actionable responses — achieved entirely within the RAG framework.

Finding 04 — Dual-Audience Value

Caretakers Are Underserved by Clinical AI

The system demonstrated particular value for non-professional caretakers — a population typically underserved by clinical AI tools most often designed for healthcare providers. This validated the dual-audience design as both feasible and impactful.

06 — Literature Foundation

Three core challenges informed design

A review of clinical literature identified the clinical uncertainty inherent in TBI outcomes, the persistent research-to-practice lag, and the caretaker information gap — all of which shaped the RAG architecture selection.

Lewis et al. (2020) — Foundational work on retrieval-augmented generation, establishing the RAG paradigm that grounds AI responses in verified source documents — a critical property for clinical contexts where misinformation carries real cost.

CDC (2023) — Epidemiological data on traumatic brain injury, establishing the scale and prevalence of TBI and its rehabilitation burden across the United States.

Corrigan et al. (2010) — TBI epidemiology establishing the population scope and caregiving context around which the dual-audience system design was structured.

Maas et al. (2017) — Integrated approaches to TBI prevention and care, informing the interdisciplinary framing of the system and its response generation design.

07 — Future Directions

Where the work goes next

Based on evaluation findings and current system limitations, four directions have been identified for continued development.

Custom Model Training

Replace DeepSeek with a domain-specific model trained exclusively on neurological and TBI literature to improve response accuracy, reduce third-party API dependence, and increase system autonomy — contingent on access to greater computational resources.

Expanded Literature Base

Continuously ingest newly published peer-reviewed articles into ChromaDB to improve the system's breadth and currency of knowledge, ensuring responses remain aligned with the evolving state of neurological research.

Automated Scraping Pipeline

Implement an automated article scraping system with rigorous quality filtering to ensure only credible, peer-reviewed sources are indexed, keeping the knowledge base current without manual curation overhead.
